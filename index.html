üìú NC Insurance Laws

North Carolina follows contributory negligence law. Minimum coverage required: 30/60/25.

  • $50,000 bodily injury per person
  • $100,000 bodily injury per accident
  • $50,000 property damage

‚è∞ Important Deadlines

Key time limits to remember for insurance claims in North Carolina:

  • Report claims promptly (within 24-48 hours)
  • Property damage claims: 3 years statute of limitations
  • Personal injury claims: 3 years statute of limitations

Common questions about insurance claims in North Carolina

If the at-fault driver is uninsured, you have several options:

  • Uninsured Motorist Coverage: File a claim with your own insurance if you have UM coverage
  • Collision Coverage: Use your collision coverage and pay your deductible
  • Small Claims Court: Sue the driver directly for damages under $10,000
  • Personal Lawsuit: For larger damages, consult an attorney about filing suit

üí° NC requires uninsured motorist coverage unless you reject it in writing!

  • Get repair estimates from multiple shops
  • Document all medical expenses and lost wages
  • Account for future medical needs
  • Consider diminished value of your vehicle
  • Don't sign anything that releases future claims

‚ö†Ô∏è Once you accept and sign, you cannot ask for more money later!

Consider hiring an attorney if:

  • You suffered serious injuries requiring ongoing treatment
  • Fault is being disputed or you're being blamed
  • Multiple parties are involved
  • The insurance company is acting in bad faith
  • You're asked to give a recorded statement
  • The settlement offer seems unreasonably low

üìã Most attorneys offer free consultations for injury cases and work on contingency (no upfront fees).

If you disagree with fault determination:

  • Request a copy of the police report and review for errors
  • Gather additional evidence (witnesses, photos, video)
  • File an appeal with your insurance company
  • Request arbitration if available in your policy
  • Contact the NC Department of Insurance for assistance
  • Consider hiring an attorney to dispute the determination

‚öñÔ∏è Remember: NC's contributory negligence law means if you're even 1% at fault, you may receive nothing!

Time limits vary by type of claim:

  • Report to Insurance: As soon as possible (usually within 24-72 hours)
  • Property Damage Lawsuit: 3 years from date of accident
  • Personal Injury Lawsuit: 3 years from date of accident
  • Uninsured Motorist: Check your policy (typically 2-3 years)
  • Hit and Run: Report to police within 24 hours

üìÖ Don't wait! Evidence disappears, witnesses forget, and delays can hurt your claim.

Avoid these common mistakes when talking to adjusters:

  • ‚ùå "I'm fine" or "I'm not hurt" (injuries may appear later)
  • ‚ùå "It was my fault" or accepting any blame
  • ‚ùå Guessing about details you don't remember
  • ‚ùå Agreeing to a recorded statement without legal advice
  • ‚ùå Discussing previous accidents or injuries
  • ‚ùå Accepting quick settlement offers

‚úÖ Stick to facts only: date, time, location, and basic details. Let the investigation determine fault.

Rate increases depend on several factors:

  • At-Fault Accidents: Yes, typically 40%+ increase (see our points chart above)
  • Not-At-Fault: Usually no increase, but some insurers may still raise rates
  • Comprehensive Claims: Minor increase or none (theft, vandalism, weather)
  • Multiple Claims: Significant increases regardless of fault
  • Claim-Free Discount: You may lose this discount even if not at fault

üí∞ Shop around! Different insurers treat claims history differently.

Diminished value is the loss in your car's resale value after an accident, even when properly repaired.

  • In NC: You CAN claim diminished value from the at-fault party's insurance
  • Your Own Insurance: Most policies exclude diminished value claims
  • How Much: Typically 10-30% of the car's value, depending on damage
  • Documentation: Get an independent appraisal to support your claim
  • Time Limit: Include in your initial claim; harder to add later

üìà Newer cars with significant damage have the strongest diminished value claims.
